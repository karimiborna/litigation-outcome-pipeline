GROUNDS FOR RELIEF

This motion is brought pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 473(b), which provides that the court may relieve a party from a judgment, dismissal, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.

STATEMENT OF FACTS

1. Emergency Medical Situation: On July 21, 2025, Plaintiff underwent major emergency surgery requiring immediate hospitalization and extended recovery.
1. Extended Hospitalization: Plaintiff was hospitalized for approximately 10 days following the emergency surgery, demonstrating the severity of the medical condition.
1. Medical Recovery Period: Plaintiff's treating physicians ordered no work for six (6) weeks following the surgery due to the serious nature of the medical emergency and recovery requirements.
1. Previous Court Accommodation: This Court previously rescheduled an earlier hearing date to accommodate Plaintiff's hospitalization and initial recovery from the emergency surgery, demonstrating the Court's recognition of the legitimate medical circumstances.
1. Ongoing Medical Effects: On September 4, 2025, Plaintiff was still within the medically-prescribed six-week recovery period during which he was under doctor's orders not to work and was experiencing ongoing physical and cognitive effects from the major emergency surgery.
1. Calendar Confusion Due to Medical Recovery: Due to the continuing effects of the medical emergency and recovery, including medication effects and the significant physical and mental stress of the unplanned major surgery, Plaintiff confused the rescheduled hearing date and believed the hearing was scheduled for September 5, 2025.
1. Good Faith Appearance: On September 5, 2025, Plaintiff appeared at the courthouse ready to proceed with the hearing, demonstrating his intent and good faith effort to participate in the proceedings.
1. Discovery of Dismissal: Upon appearing at court on September 5, 2025, the court clerk informed Plaintiff that the hearing had been scheduled for September 4, 2025, and that the case had been dismissed without prejudice due to Plaintiff's non-appearance on that date.
1. Inability to Refile: Plaintiff cannot refile this action due to applicable time limitations, making this motion to vacate critical to Plaintiff's ability to have his claims adjudicated on the merits.

LEGAL STANDARD

California Code of Civil Procedure Section 473(b) provides discretionary relief from dismissals based on "mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect." The policy favoring trial on the merits is "so strong that any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default." Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 681, 695.

ANALYSIS

A. Plaintiff's Absence Constitutes Excusable Neglect

Courts have consistently held that serious medical emergencies and their ongoing effects constitute excusable neglect warranting relief under Section 473(b). See Kendall v. Barker (1988) 197 Cal.App.3d 619, 625 (relief granted for medical emergency affecting ability to respond).

--- PAGE BREAK ---

Here, Plaintiff's absence was directly caused by:

- Major emergency surgery requiring 10-day hospitalization
- Ongoing medical recovery during the September 4 hearing date
- Doctor-ordered restriction from work for six weeks post-surgery
- Continuing physical and cognitive effects from the medical emergency
- Good faith effort demonstrated by appearing at court on September 5, ready to proceed

B. This Court Previously Recognized the Severity of Plaintiff's Medical Condition

The Court's previous decision to reschedule an earlier hearing due to Plaintiff's hospitalization demonstrates judicial recognition of the legitimate and serious nature of Plaintiff's medical emergency. The September 4 absence was a continuation of the same medical condition that required the initial postponement.

C. The Timing Supports Relief

Plaintiff underwent emergency surgery on July 21, 2025, placing the September 4, 2025 hearing date squarely within the six-week medically-prescribed recovery period. This timing clearly establishes the causal connection between the medical emergency and Plaintiff's inability to attend court.

D. Strong Policy Favoring Adjudication on the Merits

California law strongly favors resolving cases on their merits rather than on procedural defaults. Given that Plaintiff cannot refile due to time limitations, dismissal would permanently deny Plaintiff's right to have his constitutional claims adjudicated, despite the dismissal being without prejudice.

E. Meritorious Claims Support Relief

Plaintiff's underlying claims involve serious constitutional violations by the City, including:

- Fourth and Fourteenth Amendment violations
- Violations of federal court injunctions
- Property destruction without due process
- Systematic targeting and coordination admitted by City employees

These substantial claims merit adjudication on their merits.
