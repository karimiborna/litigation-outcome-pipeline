SC-108 Request to Correct or Cancel Judgment and Answer

Request
This form is used to ask the court to correct or cancel a small claims judgment. You must file this form no later than 30 days after the clerk mailed form SC-130, Notice of Entry of Judgment. Filing this form does not extend the deadline to file an appeal.

If you are the person asking the court to correct or cancel a judgment, fill out 1-5 on this page, then file it at the clerk's office. The clerk will mail a copy of the form to all other plaintiffs and defendants in your case. The court will give the other plaintiffs and defendants at least 10 days to answer this Request. The court will mail its decision to you or tell you to go to a court hearing. If you did not go to the trial, you must use form SC-135, Notice of Motion to Vacate Judgment and Declaration.

Attachment — SC-108, Item 3: Explanation

Case No.: CSM-25-870267

Zheng v. SoCal Pacific MF Management Corp. and 2900 East Ramon Road LLC

I respectfully request that the written judgment be corrected to reflect the undisputed facts and record established in open court and in court-CC’d correspondence. The defendants confirmed the apartment was vacant from May 30 to July 25, 2025, when it was re-rented. The early-termination fee of $3,620 represented about 60 days’ rent, yet the actual vacancy totaled 56 days, leaving four to five unused days, or approximately $301, to be refunded.

Under Civil Code §1951.2, a landlord’s right to damages ends once the premises are re-rented. Under Civil Code §1671(d), a fixed termination fee is void when actual damages are easily ascertainable—as here, once the re-rental date was known. Recognizing this correction aligns the judgment with the evidence and ensures compliance with California law. This request is limited to a clerical clarification, not a re-argument of the case.

I thank the defendants and the Court for ensuring that the record accurately reflects both the facts and the governing statutes. This request is intended to affirm a simple principle: compliance with California law is the standard for all, and clarity in the record safeguards justice for everyone involved.

Respectfully submitted,

Alex Zheng
