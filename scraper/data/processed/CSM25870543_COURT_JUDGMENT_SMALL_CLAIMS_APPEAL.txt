**ADDENDUM TO COURT JUDGMENT - SMALL CLAIMS APPEAL**

Kenneth Tam v. Nicholas Gregorich - Case No. CSM-25-870543

Appellant Kenneth Tam violated Section 1950.5, subdivision (f) in several respects:

1) he failed to notify respondent Nicholas Gregorich of his right to request an initial inspection and to be present at the inspection; 2) he failed to allow Mr. Gregorich to remedy any alleged deficiencies; and 3) he failed to give Mr. Gregorich an itemized statement specifying repairs or cleanings that are proposed to be the basis of any deductions from the security the landlord intends to make (the statement he provided in late September did not itemize the deductions; see Ex. 1). Moreover, he failed to return any security deposit to the tenant within 21 days as required by subdivision (h).

Appellant contends he did not act in bad faith since he was dealing with his father's illness and eventual death, his own mental health issues, and delays imposed by his chosen painting contractor. He asserts that bad faith connotes some kind of ill will and at worst he was negligent. However, appellant has been a landlord for forty years and owns thirty units. Thus, he is chargeable with knowing the law. He had received an estimate by August 5, 2025 from his painting contractor for $2,500, yet did not give it to respondent.

The court notes that under Civil Code section 3294, the definition of malice for purposes of awarding punitive damage includes despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of others. Although not directly on point, the court finds that this statute provides useful guidance regarding the definition of bad faith. Here, despite his personal difficulties, in light of appellant's experience as a landlord, his failure to comply with the law was despicable and willful and thus in bad faith. As outlined above, he violated the law in several respects. Thus, he forfeited his right to deduct expenses from the respondent's security deposit. ("(h)(7) The landlord shall not be entitled to claim any amount of the security if the landlord, in bad faith, fails to comply with this subdivision.") He owes respondent $4,550 less the amount refunded of $833.90 for a net amount owed of $3,716.10.

Moreover, under section 1950.5, subdivision (m), the landlord is subject to bad faith damages up to two times the amount of the security deposit. While appellant's personal difficulties do not excuse his failure to comply with the law, the court does find them relevant to the issue of bad faith damages. Thus, the court awards respondent bad faith damages of half the security deposit or $2,275.

In total, appellant landlord shall pay respondent tenant $5,991.10 plus costs.
