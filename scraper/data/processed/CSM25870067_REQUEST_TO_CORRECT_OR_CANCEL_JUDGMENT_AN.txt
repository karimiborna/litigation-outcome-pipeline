I, Sarah Lewis, submit this statement in support of my SC-108 Request to Correct or Cancel Judgment in CSM-25-870067.

This request is made under Code of Civil Procedure § 116.725, which allows the Court to correct a small-claims judgment where the Court used the wrong legal basis or decided incorrectly.

I. RELIEF REQUESTED

The Court's October 8, 2025, judgment currently states that:
"Plaintiff failed to prove that Luis Ernan Magallon is responsible or liable for any portion of the $11,400.00."
and lists only Ena Rosario Reales as judgment debtor.

I respectfully request that the Court correct the judgment to:

1. Add Luis Ernan Magallon as a judgment debtor,
2. Make him jointly and severally liable with Ena Rosario Reales for the full $11,400.00 in unpaid rent; and
3. In the alternative (or in addition, if the Court reaches the issue), correct the judgment to include the PG&E utility damages that were pled and proved at trial, as detailed below.

II. BACKGROUND OF THE TENANCY

I have been a lawful subtenant at 64 Toledo Way, San Francisco since May 2013, renting from former master tenant Kenneth Runyon under a lease that strictly limited the unit to no more than two occupants.

--- PAGE BREAK ---

In June–July 2023, without my knowledge or consent, Luis Ernan Magallon arranged for his mother, Ena Rosario Reales, to move into my two-bedroom flat:

- My next-door neighbor James “Tucker” Rosenbach has known Magallon for at least eight years and, for several months, Magallon was his roommate in his two-bedroom flat.

- In 2023, immediately prior to Ms. Reales moving into the premises on July 7, 2023, where I have resided for twelve years, Magallon asked Mr. Rosenbach if his mother could live with him temporarily after hand surgery, saying he would pay the rent on her behalf.

- Mr. Rosenbach declined and warned that my lease limited the unit to no more than two occupants and that I had not agreed.

- Magallon responded that he would ask master tenant Kenneth Runyon instead.

Shortly thereafter, Runyon allowed Reales to move into my unit, into the second bedroom with full use of common areas, with Magallon responsible for her rent, while I continued to pay my full share.

To accommodate four–five people, Runyon illegally converted the living room into a bedroom; in May 2024, the Department of Building Inspection issued an order requiring that conversion to be removed. In June 2024, Runyon and his wife vacated, leaving only myself, Reales, and often Magallon in the unit.

Since May 2024, neither Reales nor Magallon has paid any rent. I have been forced to pay 100% of the $4,250 monthly rent (including the $450 parking space) to avoid eviction and losing my home of more than twelve years.

D. Magallon's residence is irrelevant to his liability

At the October 8, 2025, hearing, Defendants argued that Magallon has never lived at 64 Toledo Way, and that only Reales resides (and still resides) on the premises. Even if the Court accepts that as true, it is legally irrelevant to his liability.

The issue in this case is not where Magallon sleeps, but that Magallon 100% made the arrangements and undertook to pay the rent for his non-earning mother, as confirmed by two independent neighbors. Mr. Rosenbach has known Magallon for eight years and previously had him as a roommate in his flat next door; through that connection Magallon knew both Mr. Rosenbach and former master tenant Kenneth Runyon, whereas Ms. Reales did not know Mr. Runyon before moving in. It was Magallon who leveraged those relationships to place his mother into my unit and who promised to pay up to $1,000 per month on her behalf.

Under California law, a person who arranges a tenancy and undertakes to pay the rent may be held jointly and severally liable for that rent even if he never physically resides on the premises. That is exactly what the Court has already recognized in CSM-24-869076 and CSM-25-869383, where it held both Reales and Magallon liable for the same tenancy regardless of where Magallon claims to live.

V. ONGOING NONPAYMENT AND ECONOMIC IMPACT / COLLECTABILITY

Neither Reales nor Magallon has paid any rent since May 2024. I have been forced to pay 100% of the rent to avoid eviction and losing my home of more than twelve years.

Across the three rent cases, the Court has now awarded approximately $31,744.82 in unpaid rent and utilities against Reales (and, in SC 1 and SC 2, against Magallon). None of those judgments has been paid. Reales has no earnings and has repeatedly ignored Orders of Examination.

By contrast, Magallon is employed and financially supports his mother. The only realistic possibility of ever collecting the $11,400 judgment in CSM-25-870067 is if Magallon is added as a joint and several judgment debtor. A judgment against Reales alone is effectively uncollectible.

VII. LEGAL BASIS FOR ADDING MAGALLON AS JOINTLY AND SEVERALLY LIABLE

Under California Civil Code § 1659, when all parties who unite in a promise receive some benefit from the consideration, their promise is presumed to be joint and several. Here, both Reales and Magallon received the benefit of the housing arrangement:

- Ms. Reales obtained rent-free occupancy in my home.
- Magallon obtained a place to move his mother, with continued access to the unit and the $450/month parking space, while avoiding paying rent.

--- PAGE BREAK ---

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 578, the Court may enter judgment "for or against one or more of several defendants" and may "determine the ultimate rights of the parties on each side, as between themselves, when the justice of the case requires it." Where one defendant (Magallon) arranged the tenancy, promised to pay, has income and uses the premises, and the other (Reales) is non-earning and supported by him, the justice of the case strongly supports imposing joint and several liability on both.

The California Supreme Court in DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber (2015) 61 Cal.4th 813 confirms that joint-and-several obligors are each liable for the entire debt, and a creditor may pursue one or more obligors until the obligation is satisfied. A prior judgment against one does not bar pursuit of another joint obligor on the same debt.

Here, the Court has already recognized in CSM-24-869076 and CSM-25-869383 that both Reales and Magallon are liable for this same tenancy. The rent in CSM-25-870067 covers later months of the same obligation. The law and the facts support treating their liability as joint and several here as well.

IX. AVOIDING INCONSISTENT AND UNJUST RESULTS

On the same underlying facts:
- In SC 1 and SC 2, this Court has already held that I met my burden and that both Reales and Magallon are jointly and severally liable for earlier months' rent and utilities.
- In CSM-25-870067, as currently written, only Reales is liable for later months of the same rent, even though she has no income and her son is the one who arranged and undertook to pay.

If the judgment in CSM-25-870067 is left solely against Reales, it will be internally inconsistent with the prior rent judgments and, in practical terms, uncollectible. The result would be that:
- The person with no income (Reales) is solely liable.
- The person who created and financed the tenancy (Magallon) is not; and
- I, after twelve years in my home and three successful rent cases, will likely never recover the $11,400 awarded here.

I respectfully submit that the only way to avoid this unjust and inconsistent result is to correct the judgment to add Luis Ernan Magallon as a judgment debtor, jointly and severally liable with Reales for the full $11,400 (and any PG&E amount the Court may choose to include).
